  \begin{block}{Motivation}

    Citizens rely on government websites for essential services like applying for a passport, filing taxes, obtaining healthcare information and expect privacy. Unlike commercial platforms, government portals are often the \textbf{only available option}, leaving individuals with no ability to opt out. In practice, many embed third-party trackers that collect browsing behavior and unique identifiers, raising privacy, security, and \textbf{data sovereignty} concerns including \textbf{cross-border tracker flows}. Prior work focuses on commercial sites or small, single-country government samples and is rarely longitudinal. We present the \textbf{first large-scale, global, longitudinal study} of tracking on government websites.

  \end{block}
